Complete Maintenance Guide for Globe Valves, Ball Valves & Butterfly Valves in Oil & Gas Plants

Oil & Gas, Petrochemical, Refinery

Back to Blog

If you work in an oil and gas plant, you already know that your valves are under some of the harshest conditions imaginable — extreme pressures, corrosive media, high temperatures, and near-continuous operation.

Neglect your valve maintenance programme and you're not just risking downtime. You're risking safety incidents, regulatory violations, and production losses that can run into millions.

This guide covers practical maintenance procedures for the three most common valve types found in oil and gas facilities: globe valves, ball valves, and butterfly valves. Whether you're building a new maintenance schedule or reviewing an existing one, this is the reference you need.

Globe Valves — The Workhorse of Flow Control

What Globe Valves Are Used For

Globe valves are the most commonly used control valve in oil and gas applications. Their linear flow path makes them ideal for throttling — adjusting flow rate precisely — rather than just on/off service. You'll find them on flow control loops, pressure control stations, and injection lines throughout refineries and petrochemical plants.

Common Globe Valve Problems in Oil & Gas

  • Stem packing leakage — caused by wear, chemical attack, or inadequate gland adjustment
  • Seat erosion — particularly in high-velocity or abrasive service
  • Plug/disc wear — leading to poor shutoff and increased bypass leakage
  • Corrosion of body internals — especially in sour gas or high-chloride service
  • Actuator problems — spring failure, diaphragm rupture, or positioner drift

Globe Valve Maintenance Checklist

  • Inspect stem packing quarterly — re-tighten gland nuts or replace packing if leakage is visible
  • Check valve travel and positioner calibration every 6 months — verify the valve reaches full open and full closed positions
  • Inspect seat and plug for erosion annually — lapping or machining can restore sealing surface
  • Test valve shutoff tightness annually per ANSI/FCI 70-2 leakage class requirements
  • Inspect actuator spring, diaphragm or piston seals annually — replace proactively at manufacturer-recommended intervals
  • Check for body corrosion externally — especially under insulation where moisture can accumulate

Ball Valves — Fast, Reliable, But Not Maintenance-Free

What Ball Valves Are Used For

Ball valves dominate where quick isolation is needed — pipeline block valves, emergency shutdowns, and on/off service throughout the plant. They're valued for their low pressure drop, tight shutoff, and fast quarter-turn operation. In oil and gas, you'll see them everywhere from wellhead equipment to storage tank outlets.

Common Ball Valve Problems in Oil & Gas

  • Seat seal wear or damage — the soft PTFE or polymer seats wear over time, especially in abrasive or high-cycle service
  • Ball surface erosion or corrosion — pitting or scoring on the ball surface destroys the seal
  • Stem seal leakage — a common failure point, particularly in high-pressure service
  • Trunnion bearing wear — in large trunnion-mounted ball valves, bearing wear causes excessive torque and operator overload
  • Fire-safe seat failure — in fire-tested valves, the secondary metal seat must be verified to remain functional

Ball Valve Maintenance Checklist

  • Inspect stem seal packing every 6 months — replace if any weeping is detected
  • Exercise valve quarterly — cycle from full open to full close and back, to prevent seat sticking and corrosion
  • Check operating torque annually — excessive torque indicates seat or bearing damage
  • Inspect ball surface and seats during planned shutdowns — resurface or replace as required
  • Test shutoff tightness after any maintenance activity before returning to service
  • Verify fire-safe seat integrity for all safety-critical isolation valves annually

Butterfly Valves — Efficient but Demanding in Critical Service

What Butterfly Valves Are Used For

Butterfly valves are used across oil and gas plants for large-bore isolation and moderate throttling duties. Their compact, lightweight design makes them ideal where weight and space are limited — offshore platforms being a prime example. High-performance triple-offset butterfly valves handle demanding process conditions including cryogenic, high-temperature, and high-pressure service.

Common Butterfly Valve Problems in Oil & Gas

  • Disc and seat wear — particularly in throttling service where partial opening causes turbulence and vibration
  • Shaft and bearing wear — leading to excessive disc play and loss of flow control precision
  • Seal and liner deterioration — elastomeric liners degrade with temperature cycling and chemical exposure
  • Disc corrosion — in sour gas or high-salinity environments
  • Actuator alignment issues — misalignment between actuator and shaft causes uneven disc loading and premature wear

Butterfly Valve Maintenance Checklist

  • Inspect disc and seat sealing surface every 6 months in throttling service — annually in isolation service
  • Check disc play and shaft bearing condition annually — excessive play must be corrected before it causes control loop instability
  • Inspect elastomeric liner for cracking, swelling, or chemical attack — replace at first signs of degradation
  • Lubricate shaft bearings per manufacturer specification — typically every 6 to 12 months
  • Verify actuator-to-shaft alignment after any actuator removal or replacement
  • Test disc position indicator accuracy — critical for automated control loops

Building Your Valve Maintenance Programme

A good valve maintenance programme isn't just a list of tasks. It's a systematic approach that connects your valve health to your plant's overall reliability and safety performance.

Here are the four elements every effective programme needs:

  • Asset register — a complete inventory of all valves in your plant, with tag numbers, specifications, service conditions, and maintenance history
  • Risk-based prioritisation — not all valves are equal. Safety-critical and production-critical valves should receive more frequent attention than less consequential ones
  • Condition monitoring — trending valve performance data (travel, leakage, torque, control loop deviation) to predict problems before they cause failures
  • Documented procedures — written work instructions for each maintenance task, so the work quality doesn't depend on who happens to be on shift
