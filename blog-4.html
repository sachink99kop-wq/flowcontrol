Predictive vs Preventive Valve Maintenance: Which Strategy Saves More Money for Your Plant?

Plant & Operations Managers

Back to Blog

If you manage plant maintenance, you've probably heard a lot about predictive maintenance in recent years. IoT sensors, smart positioners, AI-powered diagnostics — it sounds impressive. But does it actually save money compared to a solid preventive maintenance schedule?

The honest answer is: it depends on your plant. And in this blog, we're going to break down both approaches clearly so you can decide what makes sense for your operation.

What is Preventive Valve Maintenance?

Preventive maintenance (PM) is the traditional approach. You set a schedule — quarterly, semi-annually, or annually — and service your valves on that timetable regardless of their current condition.

The logic is simple: regular servicing prevents failures. It's reliable, easy to plan, and doesn't require any special technology. A skilled maintenance team with good work instructions can execute a PM programme effectively.

The downside? You might be servicing valves that don't need it yet, wasting maintenance resources. Or worse, a valve that degrades rapidly between scheduled intervals fails before you catch it.

What is Predictive Valve Maintenance?

Predictive maintenance (PdM) uses real-time data to identify valves that are actually deteriorating and schedule maintenance based on their true condition — not just a calendar date.

For control valves, this typically involves monitoring parameters like valve travel response time, control error (how closely the valve follows the setpoint signal), actuator pressure trends, and changes in operating torque. When these parameters drift beyond acceptable limits, it triggers an inspection or repair.

Modern digital valve positioners and smart actuators can transmit this data continuously to your plant's DCS or asset management system, giving you a real-time picture of your valve fleet's health.

Real-World Cost Savings: What Do the Numbers Say?

Studies across process industries consistently show that predictive maintenance reduces overall maintenance costs by 10 to 25 percent compared to purely preventive approaches. More significantly, it reduces unplanned downtime — the really expensive kind — by 35 to 50 percent.

But here's the catch: to realise those savings, you need a plant infrastructure that supports predictive monitoring. If your valves don't have smart positioners, or your DCS doesn't have an asset management module, you'll need to invest in that capability first.

For plants that don't have that infrastructure yet — or for valve types where smart monitoring isn't practical — a well-designed preventive maintenance programme is still far better than reactive maintenance (fixing things only when they break).

Which Approach is Right for Your Plant?

Here's a practical way to think about it:

  • If you have a large number of critical control valves with digital positioners and a DCS that supports valve diagnostics — invest in predictive maintenance. The data is already available; you just need to use it.
  • If your plant is older or uses pneumatic positioners without digital capability — start with a strong preventive programme and selectively upgrade the most critical valves to smart instrumentation over time.
  • If you have a mix of both — which most plants do — apply predictive monitoring to your highest-consequence valves and preventive schedules to the rest.
